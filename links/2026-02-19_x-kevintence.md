Every time I scroll on here I see someone saying their AI stack made them seven figures overnight. No screenshots, no breakdown, just big claims and bigger buzzwords. It almost makes you feel like if you’re not building with some hyper-intelligent agent army, you’re already behind.

This isn’t that story.

I had an idea I genuinely believed in, found a developer who backed the vision, and brought in someone sharp on the business side to help shape it properly. That’s it.

We spent months building, breaking, rebuilding, arguing over details, fixing bugs at stupid hours, refining the product again and again until it finally felt right. Then we launched it quietly in the UK just to see if anyone actually cared.

Two months later, we’re close to 1,000 users with strong retention and real engagement. No hype. No overnight miracle. Just iteration, structure, and learning as we go.

And that’s where this story actually starts.

Introducing ONTOUR: The first ever football social media matchday app.

This isn’t a deep dive into ONTOUR, so I’ll keep the context short.

ONTOUR is a matchday-focused social app. Fans post their experiences from games, see content from both sets of supporters, and revisit past fixtures to relive the moments from every perspective. It’s basically a living archive of football memories, built by the people who were actually there.

But building something like that isn’t cheap.

You need reliable matchday data, live scores, infrastructure that doesn’t crash on busy weekends, and constant content to keep things moving. There are subscriptions, APIs, hosting costs; the boring but essential stuff that keeps everything running.

Credit where it’s due, @KDudzinski04 saved us a serious amount by building and optimising things we could have easily outsourced. A lot of founders would have just thrown money at problems. We didn’t thanks to him.

That said, some costs just aren’t optional. If you want stability and a product people can trust, there are certain things you simply have to invest in.

New toy on the market, OpenClaw

I’m going to explain this the way I understood it - as someone who isn’t deep in tech Twitter/X, doesn’t track every new tool, and honestly had no idea what OpenClaw really was.

Because if I’m being real, most people talking about it don’t either. I definitely didn’t.

Then @oliverhenry posted a thread about his OpenClaw agent, Larry, blowing up his TikTok overnight and generating millions of views. That got my attention instantly. Automated marketing? No ongoing design costs? No paying freelancers to prep content? When you’re building a startup and watching every expense, that hits differently.

I wasn’t spending crazy money, but in a startup every penny matters. So I called Ollie and said, “Explain this to me like I’m five. What is it, and how do I get one?”

The way I understood it: OpenClaw is like hiring a digital operator. Not just a chatbot. Not just something that replies. With the right setup and clear instructions, it behaves like a real assistant that can execute tasks, follow systems, and remove repetitive work from your plate.

It’s not magic in a sci-fi way. It’s magic in a “why was I doing this manually?” way.

There’s a step-by-step guide written by @jordymaui on how to set it up. At first glance it looks intimidating, especially if you’re not technical. But if you slow down and follow it properly, it’s actually laid out clearly enough to get your first agent running. He has also launched a new weekly OpenClaw newsletter discussing all the latest news in the field through his agent, Momo.

Ted, My New Born Agent

I went next door and picked up a second-hand Mac Mini. Yes, I am one of them, partly because it looks clean sitting in the living room and that became my OpenClaw machine. That’s where Ted was born.

I set him up as my number two. Walked him through what ONTOUR is, where we’re at, connected him to a backup database and the areas that were draining me daily. Within minutes he’d mapped out what he could take off my plate without constant checking or micromanaging.

He asked how I wanted things structured, what access he needed, what resources to plug into, and once that was clear, he got to work.

Straight away Ted has:

Built his own skill to generate daily featured matchday posts for all socials from scratch, something I used to prepare manually a day or two in advance (recreated and improved my design, almost 1:1. still mindblown)

Created and scheduled 4x TikTok carousels, Instagram engagement posts and Twitter/X posts through https://postiz.pro/kevin-some. (used to pay someone to manage it for me as I did not have the time, and has generated thousands of views across all social platforms. Use Ollie's Larry skill as a template to customise your own one)

Researches 1000s of football related posts on Tiktok, Instagram and Twitter/X daily, gives me detailed breakdowns of what it's learnt and gives recommendations towards marketing, and storing the information for future use that could be used within the app.

Built a custom matchday tracker API for the app — automatically updates results, creates and amends fixtures, and handles backend admin tasks (this was costing me hundreds of dollars monthly through third-party services, insane cost saving)

Created a user acquisition skill — sends me a daily report of highly relevant users from previous matchdays to contact about the app (searches through hashtags, keywords, and overall scanning)

Built an app analytics skill — sends me a daily breakdown of the top posts, most active users, and performance insights across the ONTOUR app (proper data-driven reporting I didn’t have before, and honestly didn’t expect it to handle this well)

What Ted is building now

Right now, Ted is preparing ONTOUR for a proper worldwide launch. He’s structuring league-by-league expansion, organising and validating global fixture data, building scalable databases that can handle traffic across time zones, and stress-testing the backend so it doesn’t crack on busy matchdays. He’s mapping localisation needs, identifying data gaps before we enter new regions, and creating repeatable systems so adding a new country isn’t a rebuild, it’s a process. At the same time, he’s aligning marketing workflows around international fixtures, ensuring content automation scales with growth. Instead of launching globally and hoping it holds, we’re building the spine first, and Ted is leading that preparation quietly in the background.

Next steps, and what will Ted become

What Ted can become is bigger than an internal assistant.

Right now, he supports ONTOUR. But the blueprint is much wider.

Ted can evolve into a dedicated sports infrastructure agent, a system that sits behind clubs, fan platforms, media pages, and emerging sports startups.

He will:

Manage fixture databases across multiple leagues globally avoiding expensive APIs

Auto-generate matchday marketing content for clubs and fan pages

Track live results and sync them into apps and websites

Build historical archives for teams and competitions

Run automated engagement campaigns around major games

Identify and segment fans for targeted outreach

Create performance reports across leagues and regions

Instead of every sports startup rebuilding the same backend systems or overpaying for fragmented tools, Ted could provide a modular framework that plugs into any sport.

Football today, Basketball tomorrow, Combat sports, Esports, College athletics.

Anything with fixtures, events, and fans.

Long term, Ted becomes more than “my agent.”

He becomes a sports operations layer, reducing reliance on third parties, lowering entry barriers for new sports platforms, and giving smaller clubs or leagues access to infrastructure they normally couldn’t afford.

Starting with ONTOUR. But not ending there.
