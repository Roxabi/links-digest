Now let's get into the actual setups.

1 & 2: Your calendar and inbox handle themselves

Dan Peguine set up OpenClaw to manage his entire calendar. The agent scores every task by urgency, timeblocks his day, and reprioritizes when things shift. All of this runs before he wakes up.

Scott Tolinski uses it for todo management, workout customization, and recipe cataloging through text messages. On the email side, one user cut a 2-hour morning inbox routine down to 25 minutes. That's 390+ hours saved per year.

3: One agent replaced a 15-node n8n workflow

This one hit close to home. A builder set up a full-cycle lead gen pipeline running autonomously on OpenClaw. Brave Search finds companies, Apify scrapes contact info, the agent qualifies leads, pushes them into the CRM, writes personalized outreach, and handles follow-ups.

The whole thing runs on a cron job. You wake up to qualified leads sitting in your pipeline. One Reddit user said this single agent replaced an entire n8n workflow with 15 nodes and 8 integrations.

4: He fired a $30K/month agency for one agent

And that's just lead gen. Ernesto Lopez runs 11 apps generating $73K/month. His OpenClaw agent "Eddie" replaced a content agency that was costing him $30,000 every month.

Eddie automates 4 social media accounts. Researches trending formats, creates images with text overlays, writes captions, and posts daily. On top of that, Eddie handles customer support for 100K+ users, processes emails, and delivers daily KPI reports. One agent doing the work of an entire department.

5: It scripts, records, and publishes without an editor

Content automation goes beyond social posts. @VibeMarketer_  connected OpenClaw to Arcads and built a full video production pipeline.

The agent scrapes the top 10 videos on any topic, analyzes what hooks and structures are working, then pulls Reddit threads and Amazon reviews for the language real customers use. Based on that research, it writes an 8-minute video script. Arcads handles the production. You record yourself reading the script, upload the audio, and it converts into your AI avatar. YouTube channels, course content, client work.

6 & 7: Meetings and content run on the same system

The workflow automations are where things start compounding. Feed OpenClaw one blog post and it turns it into an X thread, LinkedIn post, newsletter, and TikTok script. One founder ran 4 X accounts and YouTube Shorts entirely from a Mac Mini.

On the meeting side, this setup processes every call transcript automatically. Extracts decisions, assigns tasks in Linear or Asana, and drafts follow-up emails. The smart part is cross-meeting tracking: "John committed to delivering the report by Friday. That deadline is tomorrow and it hasn't been marked complete." Matt Berman lists this as one of his 21 daily use cases.

8: Two people with AI are worth 200 without it

Then there's the scale that makes you rethink what a team even looks like. Jason Calacanis integrated OpenClaw agents across his entire company. Slack, Notion, Gmail, calendars. His producer estimates 60% of podcast production is now automatable.

And here's the wildest part. The agent runs a Saturday cron job to research best practices and upgrade its own code. It caught a timezone bug in the production schedule before any human noticed. Calacanis said publicly that the top two people using these tools are worth 200 of the employees who don't.

9 & 10: Ten agents. Daily standups. Zero human input.

And some builders took it even further. @chrysb gives OpenClaw a list of tasks before bed. The agent works through the night with sub-agents and sends a summary by morning. A DevOps watchdog monitors servers 24/7 and runs fixes automatically.

But the most advanced setup I found was Bhanu Teja P's Mission Control. Ten specialized agents operating around the clock without any human intervention. Jarvis coordinates while Shuri handles engineering and Fury manages product strategy. They @mention each other, run daily standups, and peer-review work through a shared database.

The agents built their own monitoring dashboard. Bhanu said he'd need 100 humans just to review their output. That thread hit 3.5 million views.
