The Solution: Moving Heartbeats to LM Studio

To stop the bleeding, I moved the Heartbeats off the paid APIs and onto LM Studio, running locally on Doraemon.

If you want to run heartbeats locally on an M4 Mac Mini (16GB), here is the performance breakdown I found:

Qwen 2.5 (3-4B): The "Speed Demon." Insane response times, but can be "stupid" for complex logic.

Gemma 2 (3-4B): Rock-solid stability.

Gemma 3 4B (21 sec): Incredible at instruction following for its size.

Qwen 3 4B (30 sec): The "Agentic King." It's the most stable for tool-use and routing tasks.

They aren't as smart as the full Claude 3.5 Sonnet, but for a heartbeat (which just needs to check if there is work to do), they are perfect and totally free to run.

Strategic Balance: The Hybrid Engine

This is the most important lesson: MiniMax is the Brain, but Local is the Battery. You need to balance your workload based on agent intensity:

Low Agent Count: If you only have 2 agents, let MiniMax handle everything. It’s fast, smart, and handles the "heavy thinking" without breaking a sweat.

Scaling Up (The Emergency Valve): When you add more agents, the "Heartbeats" (constant check-ins) will start eating your rate limits. This is when you offload those boring, repetitive pings to LM Studio running locally on Openclaw for $0. Use the local models (like Qwen 3 or Gemma 3) as a secret valve to keep your MiniMax prompts reserved for actual coding and logic.

The Elite Squad: The "Freshman" Rule

I stripped my 9-agent "dream team" down to a 2-agent Elite Squad running on Openclaw. But the secret isn't just having fewer agents—it's how you manage them. I use the Freshman Rule:

One Task Per Agent: I only allow them to do one task at a time. If you give an AI five jobs, it fails at four.

Treat Them Like Freshmen: Fundamentally, you have to treat these agents like a freshman who doesn't know anything at all. Don't assume they remember your project's history. Give them clear, ground-up instructions every single time.
